RE-empowering rural communities

The Sikat Solar Challenge Foundation, Inc. is a non-stock and   non-profit organization established and incorporated in 2007 committed  to bringing sustainable and impactful change to rural communities  in the Philippines through Renewable Energy (RE) Projects.

Formerly known as the Sikat Solar Car Society, the group began its  journey into RE projects with the development of the first ever  Filipino-made Solar Car, in partnership with De La Salle University  Manila. 3 versions were made of the car and the group raced in the  World Solar Car Race in Australia in 2001, 2011, and 2015.

Addressing energy poverty

Energy poverty has been defined as a general lack of access to  energy-driven services and opportunities that affect productivity,  economic growth, and overall quality of life

As of April 2018*, 2.12 million households in the Philippines are  unelectrified, with 35% of those being poverty-stricken rural  communities in far flung areas.

Source: Department of Energy

Harnessing the talent of the Filipino People

Sikat is committed to to taking a proactive role in addressing  this urgent issue of energy poverty in the Philippines. Serving  as a catalyst in developing innovative and sustainable RE  solutions for far flung and off-grid communities, we execute  programs designed to harness the creativity and talent of the  Filipino youth while promoting the study and application of RE sources.

Bathala Rocket Stove

A gift to the Dumagats

Team Carding from Mapua University

After winning 1st Place in the 2015 Sikat Design Challenge, Team Carding went on to pursue the prototype development of their project with a 1M Research Grant from US-AID.

The Bathala Rocket Stove is a communal stove features two rocket burners for cooking, and is able to produce up to 5-watts of energy, charge phones and small devices, and distill up to 2 liters of water for 25 families. This set up emits less smoke and purifies water for the Dumagats, thus improving the community’s overall health and safety without taking away their tradition of communal cooking.

The device was turned over to the community in Norzagaray, Bulacan last October 2017 and is currently in the works for further prototyping and field testing.

iLawa

Light from the Laguna Lake

Team Blade TIP
from the Technological University of the Philippines

Brgy Tuna in Talim Island, Rizal have suffered greatly from power outages in their area that affect their main livelihood of fishing in Laguna Lake. The team produced a device that utilizes the community’s greatest resource, the lake water.

The device is an electricity-generating microbial battery system that features a submersible metal-air battery turned inside out. The battery exposes electrodes to a body of water that serves as the electrolyte. A buoy holds the batteries afloat while they harvest electricity for LED lights attached to the device.

The project highlights the ingenious use of discarded materials to create the device. Ilawa’s by-product also offers the potential to alleviate the worsening algal blooms that affect their fishing.

The team has recently applied for a Fund Grant with the Department of Science and Technology (DOST) to do further development of the device and eventual field-testing in Brgy. Tuna.
